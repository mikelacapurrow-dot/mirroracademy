Securing AI systems requires more than good intentions‚Äîit requires structure. Security frameworks provide that structure, offering standardized approaches to identifying risks, implementing controls, and measuring progress. The challenge is that AI security is a new discipline. Traditional frameworks like NIST Cybersecurity Framework and ISO 27001 remain valuable but don't address AI-specific threats like prompt injection, model manipulation, or agent hijacking. A new generation of frameworks has emerged to fill this gap. This guide covers the major frameworks shaping AI security practice today and how organizations can apply them. NIST AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF) The National Institute of Standards and Technology released the AI RMF in January 2023 as a voluntary framework for managing AI risks throughout the AI lifecycle. It has quickly become a foundational reference for organizations building AI governance programs. This includes defining roles and responsibilities, establishing policies, creating accountability structures, and fostering a culture that prioritizes trustworthy AI. Governance isn't a one-time setup‚Äîit requires ongoing attention as AI capabilities and risks evolve. Map focuses on understanding the context in which AI systems operate. This means identifying stakeholders, defining intended uses, recognizing potential harms, and cataloging the data, models, and infrastructure involved. You can't manage risks you haven't identified. Measure involves assessing and tracking AI risks using quantitative and qualitative methods. This includes testing for vulnerabilities, evaluating model performance, monitoring for bias and drift, and establishing metrics that indicate whether AI systems are behaving as intended. Manage addresses how organizations prioritize and act on identified risks. This includes implementing controls, allocating resources, planning responses to incidents, and continuously improving based on lessons learned.

Key Characteristics

The AI RMF is risk-based, not prescriptive. It doesn't mandate specific controls but provides a structure for organizations to make informed decisions based on their context. This flexibility is both a strength and a challenge‚Äîorganizations must determine how to apply the framework to their specific situation.

The framework emphasizes trustworthiness across seven dimensions: safety, security, accountability, transparency, explainability, privacy, and fairness. Security is one piece of a broader picture.

When to Use NIST AI RMF

Building an AI governance program from scratch

Demonstrating AI risk management to regulators or stakeholders

Aligning AI practices across a large organization

Establishing a common vocabulary for AI risk discussions

MITRE ATLAS

MITRE ATLAS (Adversarial Threat Landscape for Artificial‚ÄëIntelligence Systems) is a knowledge base of real and theoretical attack techniques against AI, inspired by the structure of MITRE ATT&CK. It focuses specifically on how adversaries can target models, data, and ML pipelines.

ATLAS gives defenders:

A catalog of attack techniques, from data poisoning and model theft to query‚Äëbased extraction and evasion.

Example case studies and kill chains that show how techniques combine into full attack paths.

A common language to use when designing detections, controls, and red‚Äëteaming plans for AI systems.

Mirror Academy‚Äôs threat and testing content can be mapped back to ATLAS techniques to help teams show coverage and identify gaps in their AI defenses.

ISO 42001

ISO/IEC 42001 is an international management standard for AI, focused on building an AI Management System (AIMS) like how ISO 27001 structures an information security management system. It addresses governance, risk, and operational controls for AI use in organizations.

Key ideas in ISO 42001 include:

Establishing objectives, policies, and responsibilities for AI within the organization.

Managing risks related to safety, security, privacy, fairness, and transparency across the AI lifecycle.

Integrating AI risk management with existing management systems (such as ISO 27001 or ISO 27701).

Security leaders can use ISO 42001 to anchor AI security work in a recognized standard, especially when customers, regulators, or partners expect formal certification.

OWASP for LLMs

OWASP‚Äôs work on large language models provides a security and privacy guide, plus a Top‚Äë10 style list of common risks for LLM applications. It adapts application‚Äësecurity thinking to LLM‚Äëspecific threats like prompt injection and model‚Äëdriven data leakage.

Typical coverage includes:

Misuse patterns such as prompt injection, insecure plug‚Äëins, excessive data collection, and lack of output filtering.

Practical recommendations for input validation, context management, tool permissioning, logging, and abuse detection.

Checklists and testing ideas that teams can apply directly to chatbots, RAG systems, and AI agents.

OWASP resources are especially helpful for developers and AppSec engineers who already know web and API security and need a concrete starting point for LLM security.

Industry‚Äëspecific frameworks

Beyond general AI frameworks, many sectors are developing their own guidance or adapting existing rules to AI use:

Financial services: Supervisory bodies and standard‚Äësetters link AI controls to model risk management, anti‚Äëfraud measures, and explainability expectations for credit, trading, and AML systems.

Healthcare: AI guidance is tied to patient safety, clinical validation, and privacy frameworks like HIPAA, with emphasis on data governance and model performance monitoring.

Public sector and critical infrastructure: Policies connect AI security to national cybersecurity strategies, supply‚Äëchain assurance, and resilience requirements for critical services.

Cloud and SaaS platforms: Providers publish AI security baselines that map their controls to NIST, ISO, and sector‚Äëspecific expectations, helping customers build shared responsibility models.

When designing an AI security program, organizations typically start from a general framework (NIST AI RMF or ISO 42001), then layer on ATLAS, OWASP LLM guidance, and any sector‚Äëspecific rules relevant to their products and data.
