AI systems are powerful, but they are also vulnerable. This page introduces the core ideas behind AI security, why it matters, and the risks every organization needs to understand as they deploy LLMs, agents, and AI-driven workflows.

What is AI Security?

AI security is the practice of protecting artificial intelligence systems, the data they process, and the infrastructure they run from threats, vulnerabilities, and misuse. As organizations integrate AI into critical business operations, securing these systems has become essential to protecting sensitive information, maintaining trust, and ensuring AI behaves as intended.

Unlike traditional software, AI systems learn from data and can make autonomous decisions. That creates new attack surfaces that conventional security tools weren‚Äôt designed to handle. AI security addresses these gaps by focusing on the unique risks that come with machine learning models, large language models (LLMs), AI agents, and the data pipelines that power them.

The three layers AI security protects

  • Data: training data, prompts, embeddings, logs, and any sensitive information exposed to or generated by AI.
  • Models: architectures, weights, fine-tuned variants, inference endpoints, and configuration.
  • Usage: prompts, agents, tool calls, workflows, and user interactions that can be manipulated.

Why AI Security Matters

AI is embedded into core products, internal tools, and customer-facing experiences across industries. When those systems are misused or compromised, the impact can be immediate, highly visible, and difficult to roll back compared to traditional back-end services.

Several factors make AI security critical

  • AI systems can unintentionally expose sensitive data through training sets, prompts, logs, or generated responses.
  • Attackers can steer or corrupt models to produce harmful, biased, or fraudulent outputs that users may trust by default.
  • Regulations, standards, and internal risk policies increasingly expect that AI behavior, data handling, and monitoring are under strong control.

For security, engineering, and compliance teams, AI is no longer a side experiment. It is an integral part of the technology stack, and gaps in AI security quickly become business, legal, and reputational risks.

Core Principles

Effective AI security is built on foundational principles that guide how organizations should protect their AI systems. Mirror Academy uses a set of core principles that apply across all AI systems, regardless of tools or vendors. These principles help teams reason about risk and choose the right controls.

Key principles

  • Secure by design: Integrate security and safety considerations into AI projects from the first design discussion, not as a late add-on.
  • Data protection first: Treat all training, evaluation, and prompt data as high-value assets that require classification, access control, and monitoring.
  • Least privilege for AI: Give models, agents, and tools only the minimum access they need to perform their tasks, with clear boundaries.
  • Continuous evaluation: Regularly test for jailbreaks, prompt injection, data leakage, and model misuse using both automated and manual techniques.
  • Human accountability: Keep clear ownership for AI decisions, configurations, and incident handling, with humans ultimately responsible.

Key AI Security Terminology

These terms show up across AI security discussions. If you‚Äôre new to the space, keep this list handy as you continue through the Fundamentals course.

Generative AI (GenAI)
AI systems that create new content such as text, images, or code based on patterns learned from data.
Large language model (LLM)
A type of GenAI model trained on large text datasets to understand and generate natural language.
Prompt
The input or instructions given to an AI model to guide its output, including system messages, user queries, and contextual information.
Prompt injection
An attack where hidden or explicit instructions are placed in content or tools to override the intended behavior of the model.
Jailbreak
A technique to bypass safety or policy constraints on a model and force it to respond in restricted ways.
Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG)
An approach where the model retrieves relevant documents or chunks from a knowledge source and uses them as context for generation.
Model poisoning
The manipulation of training or fine-tuning data to change model behavior in subtle or malicious ways.
Data leakage
The unintended exposure of sensitive or proprietary information through model outputs, logs, or debugging tools.
AI agent
A system that uses a model plus tools, memory, and decision logic to perform multi-step tasks on behalf of a user or process.
Guardrails
Policies, filters, checks, and controls that guide or restrict what the AI system can see, decide, or output.

Real World Examples

Real incidents and near-misses help teams understand how abstract risks translate into day-to-day problems. While details vary, common patterns are emerging across industries.

Typical scenarios include

  • Support assistant leakage: A customer support chatbot trained or configured with internal knowledge reveals sensitive tickets, PII, or internal comments when prompted in creative ways.
  • Over-permissioned agents: An AI agent integrated with tickets, CRM, or deployment tools is given broad API access and can unintentionally change configurations or send messages to the wrong audience.
  • Poisoned knowledge: A knowledge base or vector store that feeds a RAG system is polluted with malicious or outdated content, leading to misleading advice or policy violations.
  • Shadow AI usage: Teams quietly adopt third-party AI tools without security review, sending sensitive data to unmanaged endpoints and creating blind spots.
